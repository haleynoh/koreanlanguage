The Korean Languge is Disappearing?
According to the UN, the fertility rate required to maintain population size is 2.08, and the fertility rate in Korea was 1.08 as of 2007. If this fertility rate continues, the "UN Future Report" released that it will decrease to 30 million in 2050, 5 million in 2200, and then become completely extinct by 2800. What is more urgent, however, is that if the neglect of the Korean language continues as it is now, it will not be long before the Korean language disappears from the Earth. Although this issue may not seem relevant at this point in time, the Korean language is taking its initial steps to become extinct.

Jeju Island
Jeju island is a small island off the coast of Southern Korea, and it is known for its beautiful beaches and volcano. Aside from the attractive scenery and the unique culture, Jeju has its own language that is similar to yet different from the standard Korean language. Jejuan, also known as Jejueo, is a variation of the Korean language and is only spoken by 5,000 Jeju islanders. In 2010, it was categorized as a critically endangered language in the UNESCO Atlas of the World's Languages in Danger. Jejuan is mostly spoken by elderly natives from the area because it is no longer officially taught in schools.
